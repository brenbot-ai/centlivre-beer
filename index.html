The Story

From France to Fort Wayne

Charles Louis Centlivre was born on September 27, 1827 in Dannemarie, France. Trained as a cooper, he crossed the Atlantic in 1847, surviving a cholera epidemic in New Orleans before making his way north through the American heartland.

After founding a brewery in McGregor, Iowa in 1850, Centlivre set his sights eastward. In 1862, he and his brother Frank planted roots along Spy Run Avenue in Fort Wayne, Indiana — founding the C.L. Centlivre Brewing Company, originally known as The French Brewery.

What followed was over a century of Fort Wayne brewing history. Several generations of the Centlivre and Reuss families — joined by marriage — guided the operation through Prohibition, two World Wars, and a million-dollar modernization in 1950, before the brewery finally closed its doors on December 1, 1973.

"The finest brewing tradition Fort Wayne ever tasted."

The Brands

A Century of Craft

From railroad dining cars to Fort Wayne living rooms, Centlivre brands were woven into the fabric of Indiana life.

👑

Old Crown

1933 – 1973

The flagship brand. Introduced after Prohibition, Old Crown became the heart and soul of the brewery. Famous for its "Lazy Aged" tagline and the beloved Lazy Aged character — a sleepy figure in pointed hat and shoes, napping on a cloud.

Flagship
🚂

Nickel Plate Beer

Pre-Prohibition

Believed to be the only beer ever named after a railroad — the Nickel Plate Road. Served in the railroad's dining cars in the early 20th century. Its mascot, "Little Nick," was an elf-like character beloved in Fort Wayne.

Historic
🍺

Centlivre Beer

1862 – early 1900s

The original. The flagship brand before Prohibition, the Centlivre name became synonymous with Fort Wayne brewing. The brand was briefly revived after Prohibition before giving way to Old Crown.

Original
💊

Centlivre Tonic

Pre-Prohibition

Marketed with the motto "Health is Wealth," the Centlivre Malt and Hop Tonic was sold as a health-boosting beverage — a common practice of the era that blurred the line between brewery and pharmacy.

Curiosity
🏔️

Alps Brau

Mid 20th century

Touted on Fort Wayne radio as "Very Bavarian, Very Bavarian, Very Bavarian Beer" by local act Nancy Lee and The Hilltoppers. A nod to the Bavarian brewing tradition that inspired many American lagers.

Regional
🍻

That's It

Prohibition Era

During Prohibition, the brewery kept the lights on by producing "That's It" — a near beer with less than 0.5% alcohol. The name says everything: you couldn't have the real thing, but this was, well… that's it.

Prohibition
Characters & Advertising

The Cast of Characters

Centlivre and Old Crown were ahead of their time in brand storytelling, creating a cast of memorable characters that gave each product a distinct personality.

😴

The Lazy Aged Man

Old Crown's most iconic mascot — a jolly figure in pointed hat and shoes, napping on a cloud surrounded by cobwebs, symbolizing the extensive aging of their beer "to the peak of flavor perfection." Debuted in WWII-era ads and later appeared on Old Crown cans.

🧝

Little Nick

The elf-like mascot of Nickel Plate Beer — a nod to the railroad theme and the lighthearted spirit of pre-Prohibition Fort Wayne drinking culture.

🍾

Quartsie

A talking 32-ounce bottle character used in promotions — one of the more unusual brand mascots in Midwest brewing history.

🎩

Crownie

A waiter character with distinctive crown-like eyebrows who graced Old Crown cans and promotional items briefly in the early 1960s.

Legacy

What Remains

The brewery is gone, but its legacy lives on in Fort Wayne's streets, restaurants, and memory.

🗿

The Centlivre Statue

The bronze statue of Charles Centlivre that once stood atop the brewery — surviving a 1964 windstorm that blew it from its perch — now greets visitors above the entrance to Halls Gas House, a beloved Fort Wayne restaurant and bar on Superior Street.

🏠

The Centlivre Home

When the last brewery buildings were demolished in 1989, the Centlivre family home and horse stables were spared — the only physical remnants of the original complex that remain standing today.

⚾

The Centlivre Beers

In the early-to-mid 20th century, Fort Wayne fielded a minor league baseball team named The Centlivre Beers — a proud nod to the city's most famous brewery and its deep roots in the community.

🚃

The Centlivre Trolley

A dedicated trolley line ran past the brewery on Spy Run Avenue. Fort Wayne residents could phone in their order and have Centlivre beer delivered right to their homes — a 19th-century version of delivery service.

🛣️

Spy Run's Famous Curve

When the brewery's massive 1950 expansion was built, the new building was so large that the City of Fort Wayne had to cut a sharp curve into Spy Run Avenue to accommodate it — a geographic legacy that remains visible today.

❤️

Living Memory

Old Crown beer continued to be brewed by Peter Hand Brewing of Chicago until the late 1980s, keeping the name alive for a generation of loyal drinkers who refused to say goodbye to Fort Wayne's finest.
